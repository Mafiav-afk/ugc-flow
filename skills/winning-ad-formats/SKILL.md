# Winning Ad Formats

A catalog of UGC ad formats verified against real viral campaigns (real view counts, real apps), plus the analyzer protocol for matching any product to a format and for tearing down any viral ad.

## The catalog

### 1. Shocked reaction + demo
**Proof:** travel app, 10.8M views, 33.3K shares (app doing $80K/mo). Repeatedly described by growth analysts as "prints every time."
**Shape:** deadpan/shocked reaction line over a face ("paris girls are you OKAY???") → hard cut to the product doing its ONE impressive thing → casual first-person caption.
**Mechanism:** the reaction manufactures curiosity before the product appears, so the demo lands as the answer to a question the viewer already asked. The product is the punchline, not the pitch.
**Run it:** reaction hook (1-2s max) → demo → caption like a friend texting. Never open with the product.

### 2. Product-as-gameplay overlay
**Proof:** study app turning notes into Subway Surfers-style gameplay — 35.4M views. Push-up app that turns reps into a game — 11M views, zero ad spend.
**Mechanism:** the product demo IS entertainment. Watch time is total because viewers watch the game, and the product's usage loop is communicated without a single selling word.
**Run it:** only fits products with an inherently visual/absurd core loop. Show the loop raw, no explanation.

### 3. Notification as the punchline
**Proof:** period tracker doing $400K/mo — 3.2M views on one clip. The app writes push notifications like an unhinged friend; users genuinely post about them, and creators built a repeatable staged-embarrassment format where the notification is the scripted punchline.
**Mechanism:** comedy dressed as embarrassment. Funny + relatable is the strongest share combo, and shares reach non-users — the only people who can download the app. The punchline also demos the product: you finish knowing exactly what it does without being told.
**Run it:** write product copy (notifications, error messages, receipts) that is itself screenshot-worthy, then stage the "it went off at the worst moment" scenario.

### 4. Mascot / character engine
**Proof:** Duolingo's owl took its TikTok from 50K to 10.7M followers, drove ~4.5x DAU growth alongside $1B+ annual revenue. A fasting app doing ~$100K/mo whose whole edge is a cartoon bear on every screen.
**Mechanism:** people skip ads but don't skip characters. A mascot can post unhinged content a brand account never could, and once known, every post is recognizable in 0.2 seconds — recognition compounds where individual posts can't.
**Run it:** the mascot lives a life (shows up places, has moods, posts "side quests") — it does not present products.

### 5. The "what worked for me" note
**Proof:** fitness apps repeatedly running: gym progress selfie / before-after → an iPhone note titled "what worked for me" → the app dropped casually mid-list → "my 3 month progress lol" caption. Individual accounts pull 200K-1M views per post with the same template.
**Mechanism:** it reads as a peer sharing, not an ad. The app is a detail inside someone's real (looking) result, so the mention inherits the credibility of the transformation.
**Run it:** the note lists 4-6 items; the product is item 2 or 3, never item 1. The caption undersells.

### 6. Spot-the-AI split screen
**Proof:** "one of these is a real ugc creator, the other is fully ai" — 160K views, 583 replies on a small account.
**Mechanism:** a question that makes people answer farms replies (the heaviest engagement signal on X), and "wait, look again" manufactures dwell. There is no product and no CTA in the frame, so nothing caps reach.
**Run it:** side-by-side, one real clip, one AI render. Ask which is which. Answer every reply.

### 7. Accidental discovery
**Proof:** grocery meal-planner app — "been shopping at Lidl for 8 years and NOW I find this???" — 1M views, format being cloned across the niche.
**Mechanism:** the discovery frame makes the viewer feel late to something, which converts curiosity into an immediate search/download instead of a passive like.
**Run it:** genuine-surprise energy, the app found mid-task (not reviewed), specificity in the pain ("8 years at Lidl", not "grocery shopping").

### 8. Trend-template cloning at volume
**Proof:** photo-to-dance-video app doing ~$500K/mo at 5 months old: ships whatever dance went viral this week as a template, runs ~320 ad variations simultaneously, keeps whatever prints.
**Mechanism:** none of the individual ads is special — the volume is the strategy. Flops cost almost nothing; winners get scaled. The app never goes viral once, it gets posted hundreds of times.
**Run it:** one format brief → many variations across many accounts/placements → kill losers weekly, feed winners.

## The analyzer protocol

**Given a product**, output: (1) which 1-2 formats fit and why (match the product's core loop to the format's mechanism, not its category), (2) the exact hook line, (3) a beat-by-beat shot plan, (4) what to measure (shares and replies over likes; saves = strongest intent signal).

**Given a viral ad to tear down**, answer in order:
1. What happens in the first 1-3 seconds, and what question does it plant?
2. Which share emotion is it running — comedy, disbelief, discovery, identity? (likes are not shares; shares reach non-followers)
3. When does the product appear, and is it the punchline, the background, or the pitch? (pitch = weakest)
4. Is this a one-off or a repeatable template? What's the variable slot?
5. Which catalog format is it closest to, and what did it modify?

**Hard rules:** never invent view counts or revenue when citing examples — use the numbers above or say "unverified". A format is proven by repetition across accounts, not by one outlier. The post that flexes the product does numbers; the post that pitches it doesn't.
